2️⃣ What will a senior / colleague actually say?

They will not attack the idea head-on.
They will ask three kinds of questions.

If you anticipate these, you’re golden.

⸻

Question Type 1:

“Why not just use embeddings / RAG / a single LLM?”

This is the most common pushback.

Your answer (keep it calm, not defensive):

“Because the problem isn’t retrieval or answering.
It’s deciding whether something is worth saying at all, under regulatory and trust constraints.”

If you add:

“This design fails closed and treats silence as a valid outcome”

That’s a very JPM-ish answer.

⸻

Question Type 2:

“Isn’t this over-engineered?”

This is the dangerous one — but you’re actually safe here.

Your response:

“It looks like more components, but each piece is intentionally simple.
The complexity is in orchestration, not in training, tuning, or data pipelines.”

Then add:

“We removed model training, embedding stores, drift management, and retraining cycles.”

Seniors hate operational complexity.
You’re reducing it.

⸻

Question Type 3:

“What are you NOT solving?”

This is where interns usually stumble.

You should proactively say:

“This does not rank insights, personalize tone, or optimize advisor behavior.
It only generates candidate insights, asynchronously.”

That shows maturity.
You know the boundaries.

⸻

3️⃣ What might you be missing?

There are two things seniors might poke at — neither is fatal.

⸻

⚠️ 1. Data Quality / Missing Data

They might ask:

“What happens if the data is sparse, stale, or inconsistent?”

Your answer:

“Agents are allowed to return null, and the system is designed to prefer silence over weak inference.”

That’s actually a strength.

⸻

⚠️ 2. Ownership & Integration

They may say:

“Who owns these data views? Where does this run?”

You don’t need a full answer, but say:

“This is designed as a service that consumes governed views.
Ownership stays with existing data platforms.”

That avoids stepping on toes.
